# Emergent social conventions and collective bias in LLM populations

**Authors**: Ariel Flint Ashery, et al.  
**Published**: Science Advances, May 16, 2025  
**DOI**: 10.1126/sciadv.adu9368

---

## Abstract

We present experimental results that demonstrate the spontaneous emergence of universally adopted social conventions in decentralized populations of large language models (LLMs). Our findings show that social conventions can spontaneously emerge in populations of LLMs through purely local interactions, without any central coordination. These results reveal how the process of social coordination can give rise to collective biases, increasing the likelihood of specific social conventions developing over others.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Spontaneous Emergence of Conventions

- Populations of 24-200 LLM agents can reach consensus through purely local interactions
- No central coordination or predefined solution required
- Mimics bottom-up norm formation in human cultures

### 2. Collective Bias

- Collective biases emerge that cannot be traced back to individual agents
- The nature of bias varies depending on the LLM model used
- Not easily deducible from analyzing isolated agents

### 3. Critical Mass Dynamics

- Small "committed minority" can impose their preferred convention on a majority
- Tipping points exist in social conventions
- Critical size influenced by:
  - Interplay between majority's established convention and minority's promoted alternative
  - The specific LLM model used

---

## Experimental Design

### Setup

- **Population size**: N = 24-200 agents
- **Interaction**: Random pairing at each time step
- **Task**: Naming game - agents select a "name" from a finite pool
- **Reward**: Matching conventions = +1, Mismatch = -1
- **Memory**: Limited to past H interactions (not full population)

### Key Features

- Agents not told they are part of a group
- No global consensus incentive
- Only pairwise coordination incentive

---

## Implications for AI Multi-Agent Systems

1. **Self-Organization Potential**: AI agent communities may spontaneously develop collaborative norms
2. **Collective Bias Risk**: Need for monitoring and guiding group behavior
3. **Minority Influence**: Key users/agents may have disproportionate impact on community norms
